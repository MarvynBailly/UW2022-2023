Course syllabus:

This is the first of a sequence of three courses at the graduate Ph.D. level on methods of applied mathematics.  The topics covered here will be useful for the other two courses in the sequence.  (The Masters level sequence is AMATH 501,502 and 503; there is some overlap of material as PhD students are not required to take AMATH 501).  Although we will start from the beginning, the topics will advance more rapidly than an undergraduate or Master-level course, and we will cover more application topics. These include branch cuts and contour integrals around branch cuts; evaluation of real integrals and improper integrals by converting them to contour integrals in the complex plane; Fourier and Laplace transforms and their inverses; Asymptotic evaluation of integrals (to be continued in AMATH 568); Harmonic functions, conformal mapping and applications.

 

Text: Textbook (not required): Ablowitz and Fokas: Complex Variables, Introduction and Applications, second edition; Cambridge. Reference: Carrier, Krook and Pearson: Functions of a complex variable. SIAM Classics.

Carrier, Krook and Pearson's book was used as the textbook for this course in the 1980s and 1990s. Professor Carl Pearson was a founding member of this department and taught this course for a number of years.  It is a beautiful book in my opinion and covers both theory and applications, but some students found it to be too terse and the exercise problems too difficult.  Ablowitz and Focus' book became available a few years ago.  It fills in the gaps and is suitable for students who have not taken an undergraduate course in complex variables, but it takes a year-long course to cover both theory and applications.  It is useful here as a reference for students to fill in the details that I skip over in class, while it also has the details of the applications that I will cover.

My unpublished book: "A Short Course on Applied Complex Variable"

The book is at a level below that of Carrier, Krook and Pearson.  It does not assume that the student has taken an undergraduate course on complex variables, and starts from the beginning.  It is much shorter than Ablowitz and Fokas. It is more focused on applications than theory.  It has a unique perspective that we will also adopt in the present course.

Grading: There will be weekly homework assignments, except for the first week and the week before the final exam.  The final exam is during the final exam week.

Final Exam counts as 30% of the final grade. The homework assignments together cover 70% of the final grade.

Homework Submission: 

Each homework assignments is to be submitted as a portrait letter-size PDF file online through Gradescope. LaTeXing (or using LyX) is highly recommended, but students can also submit their hand-written scanned work (you can use a scanner app on your smartphone to convert a picture to a portrait letter-size PDF file). The submitted work must be easily-readable, organized, and self-contained. All works must be shown except for well-known results, e.g. those in the textbook (which you should cite). The TA reserves the right to not grade any work that is hard to read or without sufficient explanation, in which case you will get a zero in the assignment.

Homework will be graded through Gradescope. Students can find the TA's feedback for their submissions on Gradescope after the grade is released (let the TA know if you can't find them).

Catalog Description: 
Complex variable and associated topics. Branch cuts, series  expansions. Contour integration, numerical implications. Harmonic functions. Complex potential (and singularities) in physical problems. Conformal mapping; applications and examples. Fourier and Laplace transforms and applications. Prerequisite: either AMATH 401or equivalent, or permission of instructor. Offered: A.
