Course syllabus:

This is the third of a sequence of three courses at the graduate Ph.D. level on methods of applied mathematics.  (The Masters level sequence is AMATH 501,502 and 503).  Analytic solution techniques for linear and nonlinear partial differential equations.  Transform and Greens function methods.  Methods of characteristics, conservation laws, shocks.
Learning Objectives and Instructor Expectations
The field of partial differential equations is one of the many topics of applied mathematics, if not the main topic.  The reason that PDEs play such a central role in Applied Mathematics is that physical problems are often expressed in space and time.  Such multi-dimensional problems cannot be expressed in general in a finite number of ordinary differential equations except after drastic simplifications.  Understanding the physical origins of PDEs and the physical interpretations of the solutions is most helpful and will be discussed in lectures but you are not required to know much physics or to give physical interpretations of results.  This is after all a math course.  Participation in class and working out the homework problems are essential. You can collaborate among yourselves on homework assignments but do not copy each other's work.  There will not be tricky problems in the exam.

 

Text: Textbook (not required): Recommended references are: J. Kevorkian's Partial Differential Equations: Analytical Solution Techniques, Springer; R. Guenther and J. Lee's Partial Differential Equations of Mathematical Physics and Integral Equations, Dover Books.  Guenther and Lee starts at the senior undergraduate level and proceeds to a more advanced level.  Kevorkian's book was written for this course when he taught it.  It has a unique approach to Greens function techniques.

Homework Submission (updated Apr 3, 2023):

You can access the course Gradescope page at the following link: https://www.gradescope.com/courses/526208

You will need to register with your UW email address (which is formatted as UW NetID @uw.edu) to access the page. If you have any trouble accessing the Gradescope page, please let your TA know (apgary@uw.edu or Canvas message) ASAP!

Each homework assignments is to be submitted as a portrait letter-size PDF file online through Gradescope. LaTeXing (or using LyX) is highly recommended, but students can also submit their hand-written scanned work (you can use a scanner app on your smartphone to convert a picture to a portrait letter-size PDF file). The submitted work must be easily-readable, organized, and self-contained. All works must be shown except for well-known results, e.g. those in the textbook (which you should cite them). The TA reserves the right to not grade any work that is hard to read or without sufficient explanation, in which case you will get a zero in the assignment.

Homework will be graded through Gradescope. Students can find the TA's feedback for their submissions on Gradescope after the grade is released (both assignment comments and in-file comments, let the TA know if you can't find them). 

Late work policy: Late work submitted within 24 hours after deadline will have a 25% penalty (i.e., max score you can get = 75% total score). The 25% penalty will be applied recursively for submission received between 24-48 hrs (i.e., max score = 0.75^2 total score) and 48-72 hrs (i.e., max score = 0.75^3 total score). No late work will be accepted after 3 days. Please contact the instructors directly before the due date if you can't submit your work on time for some special reasons.
